The research converges on a few interesting numbers:

  - ~300 words — the typical vocabulary of a pidgin language, the historical "floor" for functional trade communication between strangers with no shared language
  - 850 words — Ogden's Basic English, which claims to cover ~90% of everyday concepts. Turns out those 850 words are deceptively complex (the OED lists 18,000+ senses for them), but it
  demonstrates the principle
  - 2,000–3,000 word families — the research consensus for basic conversational fluency in a natural language

  For your use case — meaning units, not grammar — 300–500 labelled entries is probably where the tool starts feeling genuinely useful.

  ---
  Papers and concepts worth reading

  1. Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM) — Wierzbicka & Goddard
  This is the most directly relevant theoretical framework. Wierzbicka proposed that all human meaning can be expressed through ~65 universal "semantic primes" (words like THINK, FEEL, DO,
  PLACE, TIME that exist in every known language). Your project is essentially building a personal NSM from scratch for an unknown language. The NSM resource site has 1,100+ papers.

  2. Language Attrition research
  This is your personal experience made academic. Key finding: attrition is mostly an access problem, not a loss problem — the knowledge is there, you just can't retrieve it fluidly.
  Phrases and high-frequency chunks tend to survive longer than isolated vocabulary. See: Bridging the Gap Between SLA and Memory Science

  3. Pidgin formation literature
  Directly analogous to Phase 1 of your project — two parties with no shared language bootstrapping meaning through pointing, repetition, and context. Wikipedia's Pidgin article is a decent
   entry point with citations.

  ---
  Your personal angle is the most interesting part

  The attrition research finding maps perfectly onto your intuition: phrases preserve meaning even when grammar decays. What you retain in a fading L2 isn't a dictionary — it's a cluster of
   meaningful chunks tied to emotional or contextual memory. Your tool's approach of storing embeddings of meaning units (not grammatically structured sentences) is actually closer to how
  degraded language knowledge lives in the brain than a traditional dictionary would be.

  That framing — "I'm building the tool I wish I had to document what I still know of my fading language" — might be worth adding to the README. It makes the project feel personal and
  grounded.
